Introduction and Context

This report details my work for Assignment 2, which centred on the biography of Liao Huanxing (1895â€“1964), a Chinese anti-colonial activist whose life and work were documented by the Dekoloniale project. The primary aim of this task was to transform the narrative, qualitative information from this marginalized history into structured, machine-readable data on Wikidata. This process served a dual purpose: to enhance the digital visibility of a figure often omitted from mainstream historical narratives and to engage practically with the principles and challenges of Linked Open Data (LOD). By contributing to an open knowledge graph, the task aimed to connect Liao Huanxing's story to a wider web of historical data, thereby supporting the findability, accessibility, and reusability of decolonial knowledge resources beyond their original, potentially ephemeral, online presence.

My primary contribution to the group work was the extensive research and data enrichment of the Wikidata item for Liao Huanxing (Q136450200). Initially, the item did not exist on Wikidata, Wikimedia nor Wikipedia. I was responsible for a comprehensive review of his biography from the Dekoloniale website to identify key entities and properties that could be structured.

Methodological Approach

My methodological approach began with a close reading of the Dekoloniale biography to extract factual elements that could be mapped onto Wikidata's ontological framework. I developed a simple data model categorizing information into person-centric entities: identifiers (names, aliases), vital statistics (birth, death), affiliations (political parties, organizations), locations (residences, education), and skills (languages). The process of structuring this data was guided by the principles of Linked Open Data (LOD), using semantic triples (subjectâ€“predicateâ€“object).

Challenges and Considerations

The process posed challenges, particularly the tension between narrative nuance and the rigid structure of a database. Wikidata required reduction of rich biography into discrete properties. Ambiguities in dates and places created further complexity, while ethical questions arose about categorization, such as labeling ethnicity or political roles. To mitigate bias, I grounded all statements in the Dekoloniale source, recognizing that classification is not a neutral act (Bowker & Star, 2000).

Critical Reflection

This process underscored that "raw data is an oxymoron" (Gitelman & Jackson, 2013). The data I curated was interpretive, constrained by Wikidata's ontology, and shaped by sociotechnical contextsâ€”what Kitchin (2022) calls "data assemblages." As Ford & Illadis (2023) highlight, Wikidata embodies Western epistemic structures that can both empower and flatten marginalized histories. Thus, while my work enhanced digital visibility, it also risked decontextualizing the narrative richness of Liao Huanxing's story.

Conclusion

This exercise revealed that making marginalized histories visible through Linked Open Data requires critical awareness and ethical modeling. LOD can connect diverse knowledge but often at the expense of narrative depth. Structured data should complement, not replace, narrative sources like Dekoloniale's biographies. Curating data is ultimately an act of interpretationâ€”balancing the database's logic with the complexity of human experience.
