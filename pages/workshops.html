Collecting the Everyday Workshop

Completed
Date: Week 37 (10 Sep)
Theme: COLLECTING
Connection: Assignment 1 - Coin Collection
Digital Objects Metadata Quantification Photography
Explored curating everyday digital objects through data and metadata practices. This hands-on workshop examined how physical objects become digital data, focusing on the processes of datafication and the politics of what gets included or excluded in datasets.

Learning Objectives:

  • Understand data/metadata relationships in digital curation
  • Practice quantification techniques for physical objects
  • Examine the materialities of information systems
  • Critical analysis of everyday data collection practices

2

Categorising/Knowledge Making Workshop

Completed
Dates: Week 40 (1 Oct) + Special Session (7 Oct)
Theme: CATEGORISING
Special Guest: Lukas Fuchsgruber
Connection: Assignment 2 - Liao Huanxing & Wikidata
Wikidata SPARQL Semantic Web AI/ML Datasets Classifications Dekoloniale Project
A comprehensive exploration of classification systems across multiple sessions. The main workshop examined how classifications matter in knowledge systems, working with Wikidata as semantic infrastructure and the Dekoloniale project. The intensive session with Lukas Fuchsgruber focused on taxonomies and their discontents, examining how AI and machine learning datasets perpetuate biases and exploring decolonial approaches to natural history collections.

Learning Objectives:

  • Understand why classifications matter (Bowker & Star)
  • Navigate Wikidata as semantic infrastructure
  • Examine knowledge representation and data labor
  • Critical analysis of AI/ML dataset biases
  • Explore decolonial approaches to natural history collections
  • Practice decolonial approaches to data representation

3

Critical Infographics Workshop

Completed
Date: Week 45 (5 Nov)
Theme: DISPLAYING/VISUALISING
Connection: Assignment 3 - Consumer Psychology
Visual Analytics Python Plotly Power BI Data Feminism
Explored critical approaches to data visualization inspired by W.E.B. Du Bois's Data Portraits and Data Feminism principles. Examined how visualizations can challenge dominant narratives and incorporate multiple perspectives, questioning rational, scientific, objective viewpoints from mythical, imaginary, impossible standpoints.

Learning Objectives:

  • Understand visualization rhetoric and framing effects
  • Analyze W.E.B. Du Bois's pioneering data visualization work
  • Apply Data Feminism principles to visualization design
  • Create visualizations that challenge dominant narratives
  • Examine the politics of visual representation
